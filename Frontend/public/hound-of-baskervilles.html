Sherlock Holmes

The Hound of the Baskervilles

Published in 1902, the story opens with Dr. James Mortimer arriving at 221B Baker Street, worried for Sir Henry Baskerville after the mysterious death of his cousin, Sir Charles.

Sherlock Holmes and Dr. Watson travel to the windswept moors of Dartmoor to investigate the legend of a ghostly hound that is said to haunt the Baskerville family.

As the investigation unfolds, the pair discover a web of secrets, including stolen letters, hidden motives, and a dangerous plan by a family rival to claim the Baskerville fortune.

Key moments

- Holmes sends Watson to the moor while he remains in London, trusting Watson to observe and report. Watson learns about the eerie landscape, the moor’s grim tenants, and the mysterious Barrymore household.

- The arrival of a convict on the moor and the strange cries in the night heighten the suspense. Watson also uncovers a disguised spy who has been watching Sir Henry and the Baskerville estate.

- In the climax, Holmes appears on the moor and reveals that the “hound” was a real, trained animal painted with phosphorus, used as part of a plot to frighten Sir Henry to death.

Why it matters

This novel blends Gothic atmosphere with detective deduction, making it one of Conan Doyle’s most famous Holmes adventures. It explores the tension between superstition and logic while showcasing Holmes’ ability to solve a case from afar.
